introduction: "Aspalathus linearis, known universally as Rooibos or Red Bush, is South Africa's most famous botanical export and one of the most recognised herbal teas in the world. Endemic to a small area of the Western Cape's Cederberg mountains, it grows nowhere else on earth in its natural form. The plant is a fine-needled fynbos shrub that produces small yellow flowers and is harvested, bruised, and fermented to develop its characteristic red colour and sweet, slightly nutty flavour. Rooibos is the only known natural source of Aspalathin — a potent C-glucosyl dihydrochalcone antioxidant with documented anti-diabetic, cardioprotective, and anti-inflammatory properties not found in any other plant. The industry is substantial — South Africa produces approximately 15,000 tonnes of Rooibos annually, exports to over 60 countries, and generates approximately R800 million in export revenue. In 2021 Rooibos achieved Geographical Indication status under the EU-South Africa Economic Partnership Agreement — only the third South African product after Karoo Lamb and Honeybush to achieve this protection. The Khoisan communities who first used and cultivated Rooibos are recognised as traditional knowledge holders and receive benefit-sharing payments under a landmark 2019 agreement."

indigenous_knowledge: "The Khoisan people of the Cederberg were the first to harvest and brew Rooibos, a practice stretching back centuries before European settlement. They used stone tools to cut the fine needle-like leaves and fermented them in piles to develop the red colour and sweet flavour — the same basic processing method used commercially today. Dutch settlers at the Cape learned of Rooibos from Khoisan communities in the 18th century and adopted it as a substitute for expensive imported European tea. The first commercial Rooibos operation was established by Russian immigrant Benjamin Ginsberg in 1904, who learned cultivation techniques from Khoisan farmers. For most of the 20th century the Khoisan communities whose knowledge and labour built the Rooibos industry received no formal recognition or benefit. This changed in 2019 when a landmark benefit-sharing agreement was signed between the South African Rooibos Council and the South African San Council and the Khoi-San community — the first of its kind for a commercially traded plant in South Africa — providing ongoing royalty payments to Khoisan communities from Rooibos industry revenues."

quadrants:
  ik: "The word Rooibos is Afrikaans — literally red bush — but the plant's naming history stretches back centuries before Dutch settlers arrived at the Cape. The Khoikhoi people of the Cederberg harvested and brewed Rooibos as a daily beverage and medicine long before it had a European name, but their specific name was not recorded before the language and community were largely destroyed through colonial violence and assimilation. What survived was the Afrikaans name built on Khoikhoi knowledge. The name Rooibos then did something unusual — it survived globally unchanged. Unlike most South African plants whose indigenous names were replaced by Latin scientific names, Rooibos kept its Afrikaans identity as it spread to Japan, Germany, the USA and beyond, becoming one of the few Afrikaans words recognised in over 60 countries. The scientific name Aspalathus linearis was given by Carl Thunberg during his Cape expedition of 1772, with the assistance of Khoikhoi guides whose contribution went unacknowledged in science for over two centuries."
